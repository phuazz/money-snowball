Doubling starts slow, then explodes. The magic coin looked like a total loser for two whole weeks — and still won by 17,896 times. Slow is not the same as losing.

💡 What we just learned

Money can make money — and that new money makes money too. But only if you never spend the profit. Same $100, same 20%: one kid ends with $891, the other with $100.

Why one speeds up and one never does: in year 1 both earn 20% of $100 = $20. In year 2 the squirrel earns 20% of $120 = $24 — more than last year, because the profit is now earning too. The ice-cream kid is always back at $100, so they earn $20 for ever. Same money, same 20%, completely different lives. Warren Buffett calls his whole fortune a snowball.
Step 3 · How fast does money double?

The doubling machine

Drag the slider to change how fast money grows. The faster it grows, the sooner it doubles.

Growth each year
10%
Your money doubles about every 7.2 years.
Rule of 72  72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years
By age 60, starting at age 8, a snowball growing 10% a year would double about 7 times — turning $1 into about $128. 🚀
🤯 Here is the surprising part: it takes only 20 doublings to turn 1 into 1,048,576 — over a million. And just 30 to pass a billion. Doubling does not need many steps. It needs time to take them.
📄 Go and try this now: fold a piece of paper in half. Again. Again. Almost nobody gets past 7 folds — but if you could fold it 42 times, it would be thick enough to reach past the Moon (about 440,000 km). Every fold just doubles it. That is the same maths as your money.
💡 What we just learned

The faster your money grows, the sooner it doubles — and it only takes 20 doublings to turn 1 into a million. Doubling does not need many steps. It needs time to take them.

The Rule of 72 is a clever estimate, not exact. At 20% it says 3.6 years; the true answer is about 3.8 years (using ln 2 ÷ ln 1.20). Close enough to do in your head — that is the point.

Step 6 · The honest bit

Where does 10% come from?

A snowball needs a hill. Some hills are steeper than others. 🏔️

🐷
Piggy bankMoney just sits there. Safe — but sweets get pricier every year, so it slowly buys less.
0%
🏦
The bankThe bank pays you a little for looking after it. Safe, but a slow hill.
~2%
🏢
Owning tiny pieces of great companiesA share is a tiny slice of a real company. If the company sells more, your slice is worth more — and they pay you a bit of the profit too.
~10%
🧩 What is a share? Imagine your favourite ice-cream shop is cut into a million tiny slices. Buy one slice and you own a piece of the shop. If it opens more shops and sells more ice cream, your slice grows. An index fund is a bucket holding a slice of 500 of the biggest companies at once — so if a few do badly, the others carry you.
A picture of the idea: 20 bumpy years that still climb. An illustration, not real data — notice the big crash in the middle, and that it kept going afterwards.
⚠️ The honest part — this is important. That 10% is what history did, not a promise. Some years your money goes down, and it can feel horrible. In 2008 it fell by about half. People who panicked and sold locked in the loss. People who stayed calm kept their snowball rolling. That is why you only invest money you will not need for a long, long time.

Which makes the obvious answer “fine, I will just keep it safe under my bed instead” — and that is exactly the trap. Turn the page. 🥷
💡 What we just learned

Owning tiny pieces of great companies has grown money faster than a piggy bank or a bank — but the ride is bumpy, and that bumpiness is the price you pay for the growth. It is not free.

Measured over decades rather than years, a broad bucket of big companies has historically grown at roughly 10% a year on average — through crashes, a pandemic and wars. But "average" hides the ride: single years have swung from losing about a third to gaining about a third. The average is the reward for sitting still through all of it. Nobody promises the future copies the past — this is history, not a forecast.

🥷 Your money did not shrink. The price grew. That is what makes the thief sneaky: the number on your note never changes, so it always looks like nothing is happening. Nobody ever sends you a bill.
⚠️ So there is no “do nothing” button. Keeping your money still feels like the safe choice — but it is a choice too, and it has a price. The real question is not “should I wait?” It is “what is waiting costing me?”
💡 What we just learned

Doing nothing is not safe. Your money sits still while prices walk away from it — so the piggy bank quietly loses almost half its power. There is no "do nothing" button.

Prices have usually crept up somewhere around 2–4% a year; this uses 3%. Now remember the Rule of 72 from Step 3 — but pointed the wrong way: 72 ÷ 3 = 24, so prices double roughly every 24 years. Money left doing nothing loses about half its power in a working lifetime, quietly, without one dramatic day. The same maths that builds your snowball also melts it if you sit still.

For parents

How to teach this

Do not lecture. Make them guess, then show them the number. The shock does the teaching — the page is only the prop.

Ask, do not tell. At every reveal, make them commit to a guess out loud first. A wrong guess is the whole point; surprise is what sticks. A child who is told a fact forgets it. A child who is wrong remembers.
Do it with sweets, at the table — no screen. Put out 1 sweet. Then 2, then 4, 8, 16 — five days, in front of them. Then ask: “how many by day 21?” It is 1,048,576. The physical start and the absurd finish is the entire lesson, and it works on a five-year-old.
The two-cookie test. “One cookie now, or two next week?” Then: “What if waiting turned one cookie into ten?” That is compounding, in cookies.
Ask a grandparent — this one is the best of the lot. “What did a bowl of noodles cost when you were my age?” Their answer teaches inflation better than any chart, because it is true, specific and from someone they love. It turns a dry word into a family story, and it is the perfect way into Step 7.
Make it real — be the market. Match their savings: for every $1 they put in, add 20% at the end of the month. Let them feel a return arrive without working for it. Nothing else lands like that.
Let them pick the company. A share in something they love and can see — a toy maker, a burger chain — beats any lecture about an index fund.
Never let them spend the profit. Step 2 is the whole point: the squirrel and the ice-cream kid start identical. Eat the profit and the magic switches off, permanently.
Expect the boring bit. In Step 1 the magic coin loses for 14 straight days, and in real life the boring stretch lasts years. That is not a flaw in the plan — it is the plan. Almost everyone who gives up on compounding gives up during the flat part, right before it gets interesting.
